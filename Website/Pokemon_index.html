Pokemon Tier List Changes Across Generations

Overall Introduction

A childhood game that many remember or still play to this day, Pokemon, is known for its constant expansions into different regions or generations if you strictly play Nintendo's Pokemon video games. There's boundless amounts of questions one can ask about the Pokemon's stats, evolution, type, moveset, and so onl however, most of these visualizations would be fairly predictable and somewhat meaningless. Thus we could theoretically propose questions like:

  1. Are there any patterns in the distribution of base stats across different generations?
  2. Are there any patterns in the distribution of egg cycles among different evolutionary lines?
  3. Do certain abilities appear more frequently among heavier or taller/egg types of Pokemon?

Such questions take a deeper look at trends between Pokemon in between different classifications rather than simply limiting the flow of information to within the same category. This is another factor of what makes exploring Pokemon data so interesting as layers of complexity can be methodically added to make the results of the final visualization all the more interesting to decipher. With regards to our approach, using generational data in accordance with the commonly used Pokemon simulator, Pokemon Showdown, we should expectedly yield a plethora of compelling visualizations and results.

As we delve into the depths of Pokémon data analysis, we recognize the importance of exploring beyond the surface to reveal hidden connections and patterns. By examining Pokemon characteristics across generations, we not only gain insights into the evolution of the franchise but also contribute to a broader understanding of complex systems and phenomena. Through our research, we aim to uncover valuable insights that not only enrich our understanding of Pokemon dynamics but also serve as a testament to the enduring appeal and cultural significance of the Pokemon phenomenon.

Static Visualizations

Violin Plot: Complete Stat Distributions

The first of the violin plots depicts the complete stat distributions across all 7 Pokemon generations. This plot offers a clear overview of how total base stats are distributed, allowing for the identification of outliers and understanding of statistical characteristics such as central tendency and variability. As you may tell from a glance the overall shape of each violin visualization is very similar in shape, with a high concentration of Pokemon ranging between the 400s to 500s and the high being in the 700s to 800s. This indicates that overall the distribution of Pokemon base total stats do not fluctuate much. With that said, it is likely that the developers do not favor power creep and want Pokemon within each generation to always have similar stats and that other factors are favored. Some factors may be design, typage, abilities, and movesets which are all more interesting conceptually than increasing numbers.

Violin Plot: Competitive Stat Distributions

The second of the violin plots depicts the competitive stat distributions across alsl 7 Pokemon generations. The main difference from the previous violin plot is that this visualization omits Fully Evolved and Little Cup Pokemon, so that people have a better idea of how total Pokemon stats impact the competitive scene. With that said, all violin visualization for the most part have the same shape where the center area has the greatest concentration of Pokemon and narrows at the top and bottom. Analysis-wise, we see that the median competitive stats is greater than in the complete stats which is to be expected, ranging from 450 to 550 total. Something else to take notice of is that the highs and lows of each violin visualization is also not as drastic. The reason for not seeing low, lows is mainly because Pokemon with low stats would already have an immediate disadvantage as they won't dish out as much damage or be able to take as much damage regardless if they have a good ability or moveset. On the other hand, the high, highs are likely banned since they are likely legendary Pokemon or mega evolutions. Although some legendaries that may not have as strong movesets may still be open to be used.

Bar Plot: Competitive Composition

The bar plot visualization created is used to show the Distribution of Pokemon in OU (Overused). As one can see it is split by generations, and with each generation the distribution of OU is calculated for each new generation that is added. With the release of a new generation there tends to a fairly even split where the Pokemon of the new generation also rise to the OU-tier. The only sole exception would be generation 4, where Pokemon of that generation quickly took many of the OU placements. It also stays very consistent even after new generations are introduced and managed to even take the top spot on many occassions. That said, generation 6 on its release was unsuccessful showing very few placement in the OU-tier.

Sankey Diagram: Flow of Gen 1 Over time

The final of our static visualizations is the sankey diagram. This diagram maps how generation 1 Pokemon shift in tiers over the generations. Over time it is evident that new gnerations added to the sankey diagram don't cause major flucations in tiers as the connections between each node early on aren't extremely wavy, indicating that very minimal tier list changes occur. Note that there are exceptions, namely in generation 4 to 6 where the connections between some of the nodes start shifting and spliting a lot more than in the previous generations. One example of this can be seen in generation 5 OU, where the two connections that initially flowed into generation 5 split off into 4 connections. All and all, this visualization can easily show how often Pokemon leave or enter the competitive meta, but the user will still need to do proper research to better understand why Pokemon within a certain generation falls off or rises to the top of the tier list.


Interactive visualizations

D3 Diagram: Pokemon Average Stat Changes Over Generations

This visualization shows Pokemon average stat changes over generations. This visualization was mainly used to see if power creep is a common theme in Pokemon and whether stats were going up over time; however, as previously mentioned, the developers do not seem to favor this path at all and want balance between all generations. Regardless, just from analyzing the trends of the individual unique stats across the generations, we can see that there are several instances like in generation 7 where attack had a higher average than the other generations, and just overall the increase in stats are minimal and the difference wouldn't amount to greater than 10 from one generation to the next.

Scatter Plot: Statistical Influence on Pokemon Prioritization

This visualization illustrates each individual Pokemon by generation and categorizes them depending on their tiers. To interact with the visualization, you can hover over individual data points to reveal detailed information about each Pokémon, including its name, generation, tier, types, abilities, and base stats such as HP, Attack, Defense, Special Attack, Special Defense, and Speed. Additionally, you can select specific Pokémon from the dropdown menu to highlight and focus on them within the scatter plot. The color of each point represents the tier of the Pokémon according to the custom color scheme provided. This visualization offers insights into how Pokémon are prioritized across generations based on their statistical attributes and tier classifications. It complements the Sankey diagram by providing a deeper dive into individual Pokémon characteristics, allowing for a more nuanced understanding of how and why certain Pokémon transition between tiers over time. Since this scatter plot doesn't directly illustrate tier list changes, utilizing multiple scatter plots over different time periods can help piece together a comprehensive narrative of the evolving metagame dynamics.



Conclusions & Future Work

To summarize some of our main finds, the first would have to be regarding OU (Overused) Pokemon over the generations. There is an exponential trend towards the negative as expected; however, we see that generation 4 stays at or near the top from its release to the present generations as well. Hence we may consider doing more future research to better understand what is so unique or future proof about generation 4 Pokemon.

Additionally, the competitive usage of Pokemon within each generation has relatively the same highs and lows in terms of total stats, but with regards to competitive play, many players use Pokemon around 450 - 550 total stats range. This is likely due to the Pokemon of higher stats being banned, or that Pokemon within the 450 - 550s range have better abilities or movesets that allow them to stay competitive. With regards to future work, we may consider doing a deeper dive on a Pokemon's ability and moveset to better understand its impact on competitve play.

The last of the findings was in the sankey diagram where we could generalize that high-tier or OU-tier Pokemon rarely move from their positions. The same can be said for low-tier Pokemon, which is something expected. The main surprise was due to generations 5 and 6 shaking up the meta a lot as we see many tier changes as the connections in between the nodes split off more frequently. If we were to continue this project, we may consider investigating more into why this disruption in status quo occurred. Some methods may include creating more specialized visualizations to specifically ask questions about the generation 5 and 6 Pokemon.
